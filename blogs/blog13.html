Internet Ethics: Privacy, Anonymity, and Behaviour

By Kellen Strydom

In today's digital age, privacy is a hot commodity. Our personal information is all over the internet, and while it's easy to stay anonymous, this has a big impact on how we act online.

Anonymity can be a double‐edged sword. On one hand, it allows people to express their opinions freely without fear of retribution, which is crucial for those in oppressive environments or for whistleblowers exposing wrongdoings. On the other hand, the lack of accountability can lead to cyberbullying and harassment, as some use anonymity to attack others without facing any consequences.

The absence of physical contact online also changes the way we interact. When people don't see the immediate effects of their actions, they might not realize the harm they're causing. This can lead to dehumanization, where users forget there are real people behind the usernames. Additionally, without body language or tone of voice, it's easy for messages to be misinterpreted, which can escalate conflicts and misunderstandings.

Echo chambers and confirmation bias are another big issue. The internet makes it easy to find like-minded groups, but this can create echo chambers where people only hear opinions that match their own. This reinforces existing beliefs and can lead to polarization, drowning out minority voices and valuable information. The bandwagon effect exacerbates this, as people often follow trends just because everyone else is, without questioning the validity or considering alternative perspectives.

To promote better online behaviour, platforms should strive to break these echo chambers by showing users diverse content and viewpoints, not just what they already agree with. Educating users on critical thinking and media literacy is also essential, helping them evaluate sources and recognize bias. Creating spaces for respectful dialogue can encourage constructive conversations and reduce polarization. Supporting and amplifying minority voices ensures that all perspectives are heard, providing a more balanced and inclusive digital environment.

Navigating internet ethics is complex, but by focusing on privacy, understanding the impact of anonymity, and promoting respectful and informed behaviour, we can make the internet a more inclusive and empathetic place.
